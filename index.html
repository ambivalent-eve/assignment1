Q1: What was the first thing you paid attention to when interacting with the experience?

The simplistic design of the white text within the website really stood out to me as I entered the website. The way everything was moving in different directions when you scrolled down, how complex yet minimalistic it looked, the aesthetic just really spoke to me as someone who knows nothing about coding and little about UI/UX.

Q2: Spend two minutes with the experience and create a list of each of your discrete actions.

Scrolling down on the website, looking at how each different block of text moves. Moving the R around in the top left, sort of dragging it by hovering over the logo. Clicking the + button, which leads to the designers email, instagram, etc. At the end of the page, I’m using the scroll down wheel to see the different slides at the bottom, seeing how they move sideways. Clicking on the slides, which bring you to a different page correlating to the slide. Scrolling on said page and reading about the company he worked with and his decisions for the new visual changes

Q3: What part of the experience did you spend the most time engaging with?

The info page was what I spent the most time on honestly. I was really captivated by the design around the first page, and even more interested in how it was done. It gave me kind of an idea on what this specific class could look like and what I’m meant to work towards.

Q4: What was the most common action in your two minute interaction with the experience?

Scrolling down, honestly. There wasn’t much to do there on the website besides scrolling, it kind of seemed like the only thing I was able to do, like there wasn’t much interactivity in the site. It might’ve not been a bad thing, the site looks like a portfolio containing the designers work so it’s likely that there was more of an emphasis on the ‘showcase’ area of the website.

Q5: What is your impression of the intended primary goal of the interactive experience?

I found it impressive, and enlightening in a way. As someone who has little to no experience when it comes to website design and coding, it felt like I finally had an idea of what I was supposed to work towards. The showcase of his portfolio and the designwork of the UX gave me a sense of inspiration, and kind of made me curious on what I could create by the time the course finishes.

Q6: How does the interactive experience communicate this primary goal?

Everything in the website is either about Rich and why he does his work, or it's about his work and what his thought process was during it. The entire website is a display of his creations and all elements shown are highlighting his expertise as a freelance designer. For example, near the bottom of the page is when you’re able to scroll through several of his works. Clicking on them allows you to view said work and what he was doing during them. Refining the original website with a new aesthetic and why he chose that aesthetic, his new functions, etc.

Q7: What is your impression of how the experience should be interacted with over time? (For how long and how many different times)

As a portfolio, I feel like the audience that will be primarily interacting with this website would be companies or people looking for a freelancer to hire. With that in mind, said target audience should be interacting with the experience for a while to examine the portfolio. Seeing Ryan’s qualifications, his work, etc. Especially because he’s a freelancer, it’s important that people looking for designers take the time to go over his work multiple times seeing as UI/UX positions are limited and important.

Q8: How does the interactive experience communicate how it should be interacted with over time?

The website displays itself like a portfolio. It shows all of Rich’s work, his qualifications, etc. Its lack of interactions communicates that this website is a showcase. Furthermore, the information being displayed on the website all clearly indicates that this is meant to be a visual display. The awards are shown out on the top right corner, his biography in the middle of the first screen and the full list of awards when scrolling down and his work afterwards. All this information signifies that the way to interact with the website was to exemplify his work as an UI/UX designer and attract companies as a freelancer.

Q9: What other media forms (digital or otherwise) does the experience reference?

The experience heavily references digital media. It uses a wide variety of text, photography, videos and graphics in the experience. The use of videos is done within the background of the home page and his individual works, the text and graphic design is constantly present throughout the whole website and some photography is used within his works.

Q10: What does this reference/s communicate to you about how you should act when engaging with your research experience?

With the website design, it speaks to me that research experience is meant to be delicate and thoughtful. For example, his work in Bijou was meant to modernise it. By researching what the company was about (authentic french winery), he applies an elegant aesthetic onto the website. It speaks to me that research experience is important in digital media and how knowing more about what the clients want and what the product is about can help define your vision for the final work.

Q11: What does this reference/s communicate to you about how you should feel when engaging with your research experience?

Examining how Chris was able to refine Bijou’s website, it makes me realise to be open minded and considerate during the research experience, to constantly be elevating oneself with new ideas such as when he implemented the API-powered tools that allowed New Musik Radar to be able to pull from Spotify's playlists into its own music website.

Q12: What is the most frustrating part of the interaction to you and what makes that part frustrating?

I didn’t have much frustration at all with the website, not even sure if I had any to begin with. Everything was easy to navigate and the layout of his portfolio was laid out pretty naturally. I think the only issue I had was that the white text on the home screen that talked about who he was working with etc was difficult to read due to the video behind it being made of light colours and shades. As a result, it blended in sometimes and made reading uncomfortable and straining.

Q13: What is the most satisfying part of the interaction to you and what makes that part satisfying?

I really enjoyed seeing the text move around, as silly as it sounds. Something about the way the graphic design looked on this website with how each block of text was layered out and designed to move in different directions when scrolling made the website smooth to interact with and pretty to look at. I really enjoyed that, and it kind of got me thinking about how Rich was able to do it, let alone envision it. It made me rethink the depths of my artistic vision and what I could create after a year or two of accumulating knowledge.
